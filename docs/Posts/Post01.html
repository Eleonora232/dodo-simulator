Introduction

The scope of this post is to explain how we built the “Mainland” for our island evolution simulation. The Mainland serves as the source of biodiversity from which species can migrate to our simulated island. In this first example, I chose Italy as my Mainland due to various reasons: 1) its rich biodiversity and well-documented species occurrences 2) its proximity to islands (Sicily and Sardinia) which actually were a living and breathing example of what we try to model (there were dwarf elephants in Sicily!) 3) I am from Italy.

The science

This first step of our simulation is to create a realistic “Mainland” that reflects the biodiversity of a real-world location. This involves two key components: 1) Gathering occurrence data for species in our chosen Mainland (Italy) using the GBIF API. 2) Applying Preston’s Canonical Distribution ((Preston 1962)) to convert these occurrences into a probability distribution of migration. Preston’s Canonical Distribution is a way to model the abundance of species in a community. It suggests that species distribution follows a bell-shaped curve when plotted on a logarithmic scale, with a few very rare species, many rare/semi-common species and few common ones (like pests!). This is crucial for our simulation because it allows us to create a realistic species pool that reflects the natural variability in species abundance.

The Migration Engine

We use the GBIF API to pull real-world occurrence data. To turn these sightings into a probability of migration, we apply Preston’s Canonical Distribution (1962). Once the species pool is generated, we can use it to simulate migration events to our island. Each species in the pool has a certain probability of migrating based on its abundance, which is derived from the Preston distribution. This allows us to create a dynamic and realistic model of how species might colonize an island over time. After the migration events, we will then apply evolutionary processes such as speciation and extinction to see how the island’s biodiversity evolves: we are going to use MacArthur and Wilson’s Island Biogeography Theory (MacArthur and Wilson 1967) as a framework for this, and sprinkle Foster’s island rule (Foster 1964) on top.
